NOTE: On Tuesday September 18, 2018, Large Cap Trader Added to Three Existing Positions.

We have $20K set aside (13% portfolio weight in total).

Let's use half of that in the following manner --

(1) Western Digital (WDC): Use $4K or 68 shares, or an added 2.6% portfolio weight (call it 3.0% to round upwards).

(2) CDW (CDW): Use $3K or 33 shares, or an added 1.9% portfolio weight (call it 2.0% to round upwards).

(3) Lam Research (LRCX): Use $3K or 20 shares, or an added 1.9% portfolio weight (call it 2.0% to round upwards).

That will leave $10K or roughly 6.0% portfolio aside.

I have reviewed the chip story at length over the weekend. My basic view is the NAND and DRAM prices have fallen.  However, all of the bad news is priced in, and then some. This has simply gone too far, in my studied opinion. But it is not just me. Just yesterday, Guggenheim (a respected buy-side player) re-iterated a $125 share price target for Western Digital.

We got price target cuts, not earnings revisions, from the analysts. 

So, the point is this: I think we have great entry points.

Let's add weight, in the idea that an analyst upgrade cycle begins from this dire moment, from a technical perspective.

The traditiion of both NAND and DRAM is to have prices firm and/or rise in the second half of a year, due to the northern hemisphere Xmas holiday shopping effect on demand globally. That will put these new chip-related holdings on firm ground for at least six months. CDW is not a chip stock, but it is a favored Info Tech stock moving in the right share price direction. I want to add more to it now.

We can pare back later on chip stocks later on. Or just ride these heavier portfolio weights uphill, depending on how things play out.

Micron is likely to beat earnings by +5% on Thursday after the market closes (it has a number of time in a row). We know that is likely to be the case, when the stock is moving up into earnings.

More tonight!

The "Price Added" column in this newsletter seeks to match the average price paid by customers after the recommendation is made. It adheres to the following rules:
 
1)    If the recommendation comes out in the end of day summary email, then the price added equals the average of the opening and closing price of the security on the day after the recommendation is made. 2)    If the recommendation comes out in an intraday alert, then the price added equals average of the price in the email and the closing price for that day. If the editor uses a limit price, then the price added will be the lesser of the previous rule or average of the price in the email and the limit price.
 
When positions are exited, the price deleted follows similar logic as above. Your actual entry or exit price may be different depending on when you entered your trade.
